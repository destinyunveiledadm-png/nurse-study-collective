Integumentary System
The body's first line of defense — skin, hair, and nails
🏗️Key Structures
  • Epidermis — outermost layer; avascular; 5 layers (Basale→Spinosum→Granulosum→Lucidum→Corneum)
  • Dermis — connective tissue; contains nerves, blood vessels, hair follicles, sweat/sebaceous glands
  • Hypodermis — subcutaneous fat; insulation, cushioning, energy storage
  • Sebaceous glands — secrete sebum; lubricate skin and hair
  • Eccrine sweat glands — whole body; thermoregulation
  • Apocrine sweat glands — axillae/groin; activated at puberty
  • Melanocytes — produce melanin; UV protection; in stratum basale
  • Langerhans cells — immune surveillance in epidermis
⚙️Function Summary
  • Protection — physical barrier against pathogens, UV, chemicals, dehydration
  • Thermoregulation — sweating cools; vasoconstriction/dilation adjusts heat loss
  • Sensation — touch, pain, pressure, temperature receptors
  • Vitamin D synthesis — UV converts 7-dehydrocholesterol → Vitamin D3
  • Excretion — small amounts of NaCl, urea, water via sweat
  • Immune defense — Langerhans cells detect and present antigens
  • Water barrier — keratin and lipids prevent excess water loss
🔬Common Pathologies
Pressure Injury
Tissue damage from sustained pressure; staged I–IV + unstageable; sacrum/heels most common
Cellulitis
Bacterial skin infection; warm, red, swollen, painful; may have fever; IV antibiotics for severe cases
Burns
Classified by depth (superficial→full thickness) and BSA; fluid resuscitation critical in major burns
Psoriasis
Autoimmune; rapid skin cell turnover; silvery plaques; chronic; topical steroids, biologics
Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)
Inflammatory; intense itching; impaired skin barrier; moisturizers, topical steroids
Skin Cancer
BCC (most common), SCC, Melanoma (most deadly); ABCDE rule for melanoma screening
Herpes Zoster (Shingles)
Reactivation of varicella-zoster; painful dermatomal rash; contact/airborne precautions
Wound Infection
Redness, warmth, purulent drainage, odor, dehiscence; culture before antibiotics

📚Patient Education Points
  • Sun protection — SPF 30+ daily; reapply every 2 hours; avoid peak UV hours 10am–4pm
  • Skin self-exam — monthly; ABCDE rule; report changes to provider
  • Wound care at home — keep clean and covered; watch for infection signs (redness, warmth, pus, odor)
  • Moisturize — apply after bathing to lock in moisture; avoid harsh soaps
  • Pressure injury prevention — reposition every 2 hours; inspect bony prominences daily
  • Diabetes skin care — daily foot inspection; well-fitting shoes; report any sores immediately
  • Medication compliance — complete antibiotic course; do not stop steroids abruptly

Why This Matters as a Nurse
Skin is your first assessment — it tells you more than any monitor
Every room entry is a skin assessment. Color, moisture, turgor, and integrity give you cardiovascular, hydration, and infection data before you touch the patient.
Pressure injuries are nurse-sensitive quality indicators. Your repositioning schedule, surface selection, and moisture management directly determine whether they develop.
Wound care is a daily nursing skill. You assess, stage, dress, and document wounds — and recognize signs of infection before they become systemic.
Skin changes signal systemic problems. Pallor = anemia/shock; cyanosis = hypoxia; jaundice = liver/bilirubin; bronze = adrenal insufficiency.
IV and medication sites are skin access points. You monitor for infiltration, phlebitis, and infection at every assessment.
Remember This
Skin layers: Babe Spits Gravy Like Crazy (Basale→Spinosum→Granulosum→Lucidum→Corneum)
Pallor = ↓ perfusion/anemia | Cyanosis = hypoxia | Jaundice = ↑ bilirubin
Pressure injury staging: I=red skin | II=partial thickness | III=full thickness | IV=bone/tendon
Rule of Nines: Head 9%, each arm 9%, chest 18%, back 18%, each leg 18%, perineum 1%
Braden Scale: ≤18 = at risk; ≤9 = very high risk for pressure injury

Skeletal System
Framework, protection, and the factory for blood cells
🏗️Key Structures
  • 206 bones in adult; 270 at birth (fuse over time)
  • Axial skeleton — skull, vertebral column (33 vertebrae), sternum, ribs (80 bones)
  • Appendicular skeleton — limbs, shoulder/pelvic girdles (126 bones)
  • Compact bone — dense outer layer; haversian canals carry blood vessels
  • Spongy (cancellous) bone — inner lattice; red bone marrow in spaces
  • Periosteum — outer bone covering; highly innervated (pain); vascular
  • Cartilage — hyaline (joints), fibrocartilage (discs), elastic (ear/epiglottis)
  • Ligaments — bone to bone | Tendons — muscle to bone
  • Joint types — synarthrosis (immovable), amphiarthrosis (slightly movable), diarthrosis (freely movable)
⚙️Function Summary
  • Support — structural framework; maintains body shape
  • Protection — skull→brain; vertebrae→spinal cord; ribs→heart/lungs
  • Movement — levers for muscle attachment
  • Hematopoiesis — red blood cell production in red bone marrow
  • Mineral storage — 99% of body calcium; 85% of phosphorus stored in bone
  • Fat storage — yellow bone marrow stores adipose tissue
  • Hormone production — osteocalcin regulates insulin secretion and energy metabolism
🔬Common Pathologies
Osteoporosis
↓ bone density; silent until fracture; hip/vertebral fractures common; bisphosphonates + calcium/Vit D
Fractures
Break in bone continuity; types: closed, open, greenstick, comminuted, stress; 5 P's of compartment syndrome
Osteoarthritis
Wear-and-tear cartilage degeneration; pain with use; joint stiffness; most common joint disease
Rheumatoid Arthritis
Autoimmune synovial inflammation; morning stiffness >1hr; symmetric joints; DMARDs/biologics
Osteomyelitis
Bone infection; S. aureus most common; localized pain, fever, ↑ WBC; IV antibiotics 4–6 weeks
Gout
Uric acid crystal deposits in joints; great toe (podagra); acute pain/swelling; allopurinol/colchicine
Bone Cancer
Primary (osteosarcoma) or metastatic; pain worse at night; pathological fractures; chemo/surgery
Compartment Syndrome
↑ pressure in fascial compartment; emergency; 5 P's; fasciotomy may be required

📚Patient Education Points
  • Calcium and Vitamin D intake — 1000–1200mg calcium/day; 600–800 IU Vit D/day; dairy, leafy greens, fortified foods
  • Weight-bearing exercise — walking, dancing, resistance training builds bone density
  • Fall prevention — remove rugs, grab bars in bathroom, adequate lighting, review medications that cause dizziness

  • Joint protection for arthritis — use assistive devices; avoid repetitive stress; proper body mechanics
  • Cast/splint care — keep dry; do not insert objects to scratch; report numbness, tingling, increased pain
  • Report warning signs — severe pain, new swelling, loss of function — could indicate fracture or compartment syndrome

Why This Matters as a Nurse
Bones break, calcium goes wrong, and falls happen — you are the prevention
Fall prevention is a top nursing priority. Understanding bone fragility — especially in elderly, osteoporotic, or steroid-treated patients — shapes every mobility and safety assessment.
Fractures require neurovascular monitoring. The 6 P's (pain, pressure, paralysis, paresthesia, pallor, pulselessness) are your post-injury assessment framework.
Calcium imbalances cause life-threatening symptoms. You need to recognize Chvostek's sign, Trousseau's sign, and know which medications cause hypocalcemia.
Post-surgical orthopedic care is complex. Hip/knee replacements require specific positioning restrictions, DVT prophylaxis, and early mobility protocols.
Bone marrow suppression affects your most vulnerable patients. Chemotherapy and radiation impair hematopoiesis — you monitor for pancytopenia complications.

Muscular System
Movement, posture, and heat generation
🏗️Key Structures
  • Skeletal muscle — voluntary; striated; attached to bones via tendons; ~640 muscles
  • Smooth muscle — involuntary; non-striated; walls of hollow organs and vessels
  • Cardiac muscle — involuntary; striated; only in heart; intercalated discs for signal conduction
  • Sarcomere — basic contractile unit; actin (thin) + myosin (thick) filaments
  • Neuromuscular junction (NMJ) — synapse between motor neuron and muscle fiber; ACh released
  • Motor unit — one motor neuron + all muscle fibers it innervates
  • Fascia — connective tissue surrounding muscles; compartments
  • Origin — proximal/fixed attachment | Insertion — distal/moving attachment
⚙️Function Summary
  • Movement — locomotion, breathing, swallowing, eye movement
  • Posture — constant low-level contraction maintains body position
  • Heat production — 85% of body heat generated by skeletal muscle activity
  • Respiration — diaphragm (phrenic nerve C3–C5) + intercostals enable ventilation
  • Peristalsis — smooth muscle moves food and waste through GI tract
  • Circulation — smooth muscle in vessel walls regulates blood pressure
  • Protection — abdominal muscles protect internal organs
🔬Common Pathologies
Muscle Strain
Overstretching or tearing; grades I–III; RICE (rest, ice, compression, elevation)
Rhabdomyolysis
Muscle breakdown → myoglobin released → renal tubule damage; ↑ CK; dark urine; aggressive IV fluids
Fibromyalgia
Widespread musculoskeletal pain + fatigue; tender points; no structural damage; multimodal treatment
Muscular Dystrophy
Genetic progressive muscle wasting; Duchenne most common (X-linked); cardiac/respiratory involvement
Myasthenia Gravis
Autoimmune; ACh receptor antibodies; fatigable muscle weakness; ptosis; respiratory crisis
Compartment Syndrome
↑ pressure in fascial compartment; ischemia; 5 P's; surgical emergency
Polymyositis/Dermatomyositis
Autoimmune muscle inflammation; proximal weakness; skin changes (dermatomyositis); steroids
Malignant Hyperthermia
Rare life-threatening reaction to anesthesia; hyperthermia, rigidity; dantrolene sodium

📚Patient Education Points
  • Proper warm-up and cool-down — prevents strains and delayed onset muscle soreness
  • Hydration — critical for muscle function; dehydration increases cramp risk
  • Statin awareness — report unexplained muscle pain, weakness, or dark urine to provider immediately
  • Progressive exercise after injury — return to activity gradually; listen to the body
  • Body mechanics — lift with legs not back; use assistive devices; prevent occupational injuries
  • Myasthenia Gravis patients — avoid heat, stress, and overexertion; recognize myasthenic crisis signs; carry medical ID
  • Electrolyte-rich diet — bananas (K+), dairy (Ca2+), nuts/seeds (Mg2+) support muscle function

Why This Matters as a Nurse
Muscle function = patient function — immobility is one of nursing's biggest enemies
Early ambulation is one of your most powerful nursing interventions. Within days of bed rest, muscle atrophy begins — you prevent it with scheduled mobility and ROM exercises.
Electrolyte imbalances manifest in muscles first. Weakness, cramps, and tetany from K+, Ca2+, and Mg2+ abnormalities are things you assess, report, and treat.
Respiratory muscles can fail. The diaphragm is a muscle — Guillain-Barré, cervical SCI, and severe hypokalemia can cause respiratory failure from muscle weakness.
Rhabdomyolysis can cause acute kidney injury. Extreme exertion, crush injuries, or statins can cause massive muscle breakdown — monitor CK, urine color, and renal function.
Fall prevention involves muscle assessment. Muscle strength, gait stability, and balance directly determine fall risk — your assessment drives the care plan.
Remember This
Skeletal = voluntary | Smooth = organs | Cardiac = heart only | All need Ca2+ to contract

Sliding filament: myosin heads pull actin; needs ATP + Ca2+
Rhabdo triad: muscle pain + weakness + dark urine → check CK + renal function
Malignant hyperthermia: triggered by succinylcholine/volatile anesthetics → dantrolene STAT

Nervous System
The body's command and communication center
🏗️Key Structures
  • CNS — brain + spinal cord; protected by meninges and CSF
  • PNS — 12 cranial nerve pairs + 31 spinal nerve pairs
  • Cerebrum — 4 lobes: frontal (executive/motor), parietal (sensory/spatial), temporal (hearing/memory), occipital (vision)
  • Cerebellum — coordination, balance, fine motor control
  • Brainstem — midbrain, pons, medulla; vital life functions (RR, HR, BP)
  • Limbic system — emotion, motivation, memory (hippocampus, amygdala)
  • Neuron — dendrites (receive) → cell body → axon (transmit); synapse with neurotransmitters
  • Sympathetic (SNS) — fight or flight; T1–L2 | Parasympathetic (PNS) — rest and digest; cranial and sacral
  • BBB — blood-brain barrier; selective; protects brain from pathogens/toxins
⚙️Function Summary
  • Sensory input — receives info from internal and external environment
  • Integration — processes, interprets, stores sensory data
  • Motor output — initiates voluntary and involuntary responses
  • Higher functions — thought, consciousness, language, memory, personality
  • Homeostasis regulation — autonomic control of HR, BP, respirations, temperature, digestion
  • Reflex arcs — rapid spinal responses without brain processing
  • Endocrine coordination — hypothalamus bridges nervous and endocrine systems
🔬Common Pathologies
Stroke (CVA)
Ischemic (clot, 85%) or hemorrhagic (bleed); FAST; tPA within 4.5h for ischemic; time = brain
Seizures
Abnormal electrical discharge; generalized (tonic-clonic) vs focal; airway/safety priority; anticonvulsants
Meningitis
Infection of meninges; Kernig's + Brudzinski's signs; nuchal rigidity; fever; LP for diagnosis; isolation
Increased ICP
↑ pressure in skull; Cushing's triad (HTN+bradycardia+irregular RR); HOB 30°; hyperosmolar therapy
Spinal Cord Injury
Level determines deficit; cervical→quadriplegia; thoracic→paraplegia; neurogenic shock; autonomic dysreflexia
Parkinson's Disease
Dopamine deficiency; TRAP (tremor, rigidity, akinesia, postural instability); levodopa/carbidopa
Multiple Sclerosis
Demyelination; relapsing-remitting; fatigue, weakness, vision changes; disease-modifying therapies
Guillain-Barré
Ascending paralysis; autoimmune demyelination; respiratory failure risk; IVIG or plasmapheresis

Why This Matters as a Nurse
The nervous system changes fast — and you are the one at the bedside catching it
Neuro changes are the first sign of deterioration. A subtle LOC change, new confusion, or personality shift can signal stroke, sepsis, ICP, or medication toxicity — you catch it first.
You perform neuro checks more than any other provider. GCS, pupils, motor/sensory — every shift, every hour in critical care. Your documentation is the trend data.
Stroke is a time-sensitive emergency. Every minute of delay destroys ~1.9 million neurons. Your FAST recognition and rapid response directly saves lives and function.
Pain is a nervous system phenomenon. Understanding pain pathways helps you assess accurately, choose interventions, and advocate for adequate management.
Autonomic dysreflexia in SCI patients above T6 is a life-threatening emergency. You must recognize triggers (full bladder, pressure), symptoms (hypertensive crisis, headache), and respond immediately.

Endocrine System
Chemical messengers that regulate metabolism and homeostasis
🏗️Key Structures
  • Hypothalamus — master regulator; releases/inhibits pituitary hormones
  • Anterior pituitary — GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, Prolactin ("FLAT PiG")
  • Posterior pituitary — ADH (vasopressin), Oxytocin (stored; produced in hypothalamus)
  • Thyroid — T3 (active), T4 (storage); Calcitonin (↓ Ca2+)
  • Parathyroid — 4 glands; PTH (↑ Ca2+ when levels low)
  • Adrenal cortex — Cortisol (stress), Aldosterone (Na+/K+), Androgens (sex hormones)
  • Adrenal medulla — Epinephrine 80%, Norepinephrine 20% (fight-or-flight)
  • Pancreas (endocrine) — Beta cells→Insulin (↓ glucose); Alpha cells→Glucagon (↑ glucose)
  • Pineal gland — Melatonin; circadian rhythm regulation
⚙️Function Summary
  • Metabolism — thyroid hormones set metabolic rate for every cell
  • Blood glucose homeostasis — insulin (↓) vs glucagon (↑) balance
  • Stress response — cortisol + epinephrine mobilize energy, suppress inflammation
  • Fluid/electrolyte balance — ADH (water), aldosterone (Na+/K+)
  • Growth and development — GH, thyroid hormones, sex hormones
  • Reproduction — FSH, LH, estrogen, progesterone, testosterone
  • Calcium regulation — PTH (↑) vs calcitonin (↓); bone, kidney, GI all respond

Autoimmune beta cell destruction; no insulin production; ketosis-prone; insulin-dependent for life

Insulin resistance + relative deficiency; lifestyle + oral agents + insulin; most common endocrine disorder

Why This Matters as a Nurse
Hormones regulate everything — endocrine emergencies are life-threatening and you are first to see them
Diabetes management is a daily nursing responsibility. Blood glucose monitoring, insulin administration, carb counting, and hypoglycemia treatment are core skills used every shift.
Endocrine emergencies develop fast. DKA, HHS, thyroid storm, adrenal crisis, and severe hypoglycemia all require immediate nursing recognition and intervention — minutes matter.
Stress physiology affects every hospitalized patient. Illness spikes cortisol and disrupts glucose — your diabetic patients need more frequent monitoring when acutely ill.
Many common medications affect the endocrine system. Corticosteroids raise glucose; beta-blockers mask hypoglycemia signs; lithium affects thyroid — you monitor for these interactions.
Patient education is critical for chronic endocrine conditions. Diabetes, hypothyroidism, and adrenal insufficiency are lifelong — your teaching directly determines their safety at home.
Remember This

Cardiovascular System
Oxygen, nutrients, and waste transported through 60,000 miles of vessels
🏗️Key Structures
  • Heart chambers — RA (receives systemic venous blood) → RV → lungs → LA → LV → body
  • Valves (TPMA) — Tricuspid (RA→RV), Pulmonic (RV→PA), Mitral (LA→LV), Aortic (LV→Aorta)
  • Conduction system — SA node (60–100 bpm) → AV node (40–60) → Bundle of His → Bundle branches → Purkinje (20–40)
  • Pericardium — fibrous outer + serous inner layers; pericardial space (15–50 mL fluid)
  • Coronary arteries — RCA (right ventricle, inferior LV), LAD (anterior LV), LCx (lateral LV)
  • Arteries — carry blood away from heart; elastic (aorta) + muscular; high pressure
  • Veins — carry blood toward heart; low pressure; have valves to prevent backflow
  • Capillaries — 1-cell thick; site of gas/nutrient/waste exchange; connect arterioles to venules
⚙️Function Summary
  • O2 and nutrient transport — delivers to every cell; CO2 and waste removed
  • Pulmonary circulation — right heart → pulmonary arteries → lungs → pulmonary veins → left heart
  • Systemic circulation — left heart → aorta → body → vena cava → right heart
  • Blood pressure maintenance — cardiac output × systemic vascular resistance
  • Thermoregulation — cutaneous blood flow regulates heat loss
  • Immune cell transport — WBCs, antibodies, complement circulate
  • Hemostasis — platelets + clotting factors respond to vessel injury

Why This Matters as a Nurse
The cardiovascular system is at the center of almost every clinical emergency you will face
Cardiovascular assessment is performed every single shift. Heart sounds, peripheral pulses, capillary refill, edema, and BP — these are your baseline data points for every patient.

Lymphatic / Immune System
Fluid balance, pathogen defense, and immune surveillance
🏗️Key Structures
  • Lymphatic vessels — parallel venous system; one-way flow; lacteals in GI absorb fat
  • Lymph nodes — bean-shaped filters; concentrated in neck, axilla, groin, mesentery
  • Spleen — filters blood; destroys old RBCs; immune activation; largest lymphoid organ
  • Thymus — T-cell maturation; largest in childhood; involutes after puberty
  • Tonsils/Peyer's patches/Appendix — MALT (mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue)
  • T lymphocytes — cell-mediated immunity; helper (CD4+), cytotoxic (CD8+), regulatory
  • B lymphocytes — humoral immunity; differentiate into plasma cells → antibodies
  • NK cells — innate; kill cancer cells and virus-infected cells without prior sensitization
  • Macrophages/Dendritic cells — phagocytosis + antigen presentation to T cells
⚙️Function Summary
  • Fluid balance — returns ~3L/day of interstitial fluid to bloodstream
  • Fat absorption — lacteals absorb dietary fats → chylomicrons → thoracic duct → blood
  • Innate immunity — nonspecific, immediate: inflammation, fever, phagocytosis, NK cells
  • Adaptive immunity — specific, delayed, memory: T cells (cell-mediated) + B cells (humoral)
  • Antibody production — B cells produce IgG (most common), IgM (first), IgA (secretions), IgE (allergy), IgD
  • Immune surveillance — identifies and destroys abnormal cells (cancer surveillance)
  • Inflammatory response — redness, heat, swelling, pain = cardinal signs of inflammation

Respiratory System
Gas exchange — the bridge between air and blood
🏗️Key Structures
  • Upper airway — nose, sinuses, pharynx, larynx; filters, warms, humidifies air
  • Trachea — 10–12 cm; C-shaped cartilage rings; divides at carina (T4–T5)
  • Right main bronchus — shorter, wider, more vertical; aspiration goes right
  • Bronchi → Bronchioles — progressive branching; bronchioles lack cartilage; bronchospasm possible
  • Alveoli — ~300 million; Type I pneumocytes (structure) + Type II (surfactant production)
  • Surfactant — phospholipid; ↓ surface tension; prevents alveolar collapse on expiration
  • Diaphragm — primary breathing muscle; phrenic nerve (C3–C4–C5); "C3, 4, 5 keep you alive"
  • Pleura — visceral (lung surface) + parietal (chest wall); pleural space normally ~5–10 mL fluid
  • Alveolar-capillary membrane — 0.5 µm thick; site of diffusion; O2 in, CO2 out
⚙️Function Summary
  • Ventilation — moving air in (inspiration) and out (expiration)
  • Gas exchange (respiration) — O2 enters blood; CO2 exits at alveolar-capillary membrane
  • O2 transport — 98% bound to hemoglobin; 2% dissolved in plasma
  • CO2 elimination — 70% as bicarbonate; 23% bound to hemoglobin; 7% dissolved
  • pH regulation — CO2 + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 ⇌ H+ + HCO3- (respiratory acid-base balance)
  • Protection — cilia + mucus trap particles; cough/sneeze reflexes
  • Vocalization and olfaction — larynx produces sound; nasal epithelium detects smell

Why This Matters as a Nurse
Breathing is life — respiratory failure can occur in minutes and you are the first responder
Respiratory assessment is your most time-sensitive assessment. Rate, depth, rhythm, effort, SpO2, and breath sounds — abnormalities here require immediate action.
Airway is always your first priority in any emergency. No matter what else is wrong, airway comes before breathing before circulation — you manage it at the bedside.

Breaking down food into fuel the body can actually use
🏗️Key Structures
  • Mouth — mechanical digestion (teeth); chemical: salivary amylase (starch), lingual lipase (fat)
  • Esophagus — 25 cm; peristalsis; UES and LES (prevents reflux)

  • Elimination — waste compacted and expelled as feces

Urinary System
The body's filtration plant — waste out, balance maintained
🏗️Key Structures
  • Kidneys — retroperitoneal; right lower than left (liver); 12 cm × 6 cm; ~150g each
  • Nephron — functional unit; ~1 million per kidney; filtration + reabsorption + secretion
  • Glomerulus — capillary knot inside Bowman's capsule; filtration; GFR ~125 mL/min
  • Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) — reabsorbs 65% of filtrate; glucose, Na+, HCO3-
  • Loop of Henle — descending (water permeable), ascending (impermeable); concentration gradient
  • Distal convoluted tubule (DCT) — aldosterone acts here; fine-tune Na+/K+ exchange
  • Collecting duct — ADH acts here; final water reabsorption; urine concentration
  • Ureters, bladder (350–500 mL capacity), urethra — transport and storage
⚙️Function Summary
  • Filtration — 180L filtered daily; 99% reabsorbed → only ~1.5L excreted
  • Waste excretion — urea (protein breakdown), creatinine (muscle), uric acid, drugs
  • Fluid balance — adjusts water excretion based on ADH signaling
  • Electrolyte regulation — Na+, K+, Ca2+, Mg2+, phosphorus levels maintained
  • Acid-base balance — excretes H+ ions; reabsorbs HCO3-; metabolic compensation
  • Hormone production — erythropoietin (RBC production), renin (BP), calcitriol (Vit D activation)
  • Blood pressure regulation — RAAS; water/sodium volume control

Why This Matters as a Nurse
Fluid balance, kidney function, and catheter care are central to daily nursing practice
Intake and output monitoring is a nursing responsibility. You measure, document, and respond to fluid imbalances — dehydration and overload both cause serious complications.
CAUTIs are largely preventable and nurses determine outcomes. Your sterile insertion technique, daily catheter care, and early removal decisions directly reduce infection rates.
Kidney function determines drug dosing for almost every medication. Understanding GFR and creatinine clearance helps you recognize dangerous orders and prevent nephrotoxicity.
Urine is a diagnostic window. Color, clarity, output, specific gravity, and odor all provide clinical data — your observation and reporting drives workup.
AKI is often preventable. Adequate hydration, avoiding nephrotoxic drugs, monitoring urine output, and early sepsis recognition are nursing interventions that protect kidney function.

Reproductive System
Continuation of the species — hormones, pregnancy, and complex care
🏗️Key Structures
  • Female — ovaries — produce eggs; estrogen + progesterone production; follicles develop monthly
  • Fallopian tubes — site of fertilization; cilia sweep egg toward uterus; ectopic pregnancy risk
  • Uterus — endometrium (lining), myometrium (muscle), perimetrium (outer); grows 1cm/week in pregnancy
  • Cervix — lower uterus; dilates in labor; Pap smear site; mucus plug in pregnancy
  • Male — testes — produce sperm (spermatogenesis) + testosterone; in scrotum (2°C cooler than body)
  • Epididymis — sperm maturation and storage; site of sperm motility development
  • Vas deferens — sperm transport from epididymis to ejaculatory duct
  • Prostate gland — secretes seminal fluid; surrounds urethra; enlarges with age (BPH)
  • Placenta — gas/nutrient/waste exchange; produces hCG, estrogen, progesterone, HPL
⚙️Function Summary
  • Gamete production — oogenesis (eggs) in females; spermatogenesis (sperm) in males
  • Hormone production — estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, hCG, inhibin
  • Fertilization — sperm + egg → zygote; occurs in ampulla of fallopian tube
  • Implantation — blastocyst embeds in endometrium; hCG produced → positive pregnancy test
  • Gestation — 3 trimesters; 40 weeks (280 days) from LMP
  • Parturition (labor) — oxytocin + prostaglandins drive uterine contractions; 3 stages
  • Lactation — prolactin (milk production) + oxytocin (let-down reflex)
  • Menstrual cycle — ~28 days; follicular phase → ovulation (day 14) → luteal phase
🔬Common Pathologies
Ectopic Pregnancy
Implantation outside uterus (usually tube); hCG ↑ + pain + no IUP on US; rupture = hemorrhage; methotrexate or surgery
Preeclampsia
HTN + proteinuria after 20 weeks; severe features: BP ≥160/110; MgSO4 for seizure prevention; delivery is cure
Eclampsia
Seizures in preeclampsia/postpartum; MgSO4; side rails up; O2; protect airway; emergency delivery
Postpartum Hemorrhage
>500 mL vaginal or >1000 mL C-section blood loss; uterine atony most common; HAEMOSTASIS mnemonic
Placenta Previa
Placenta covers cervical os; painless bright red bleeding; NO vaginal exams; C-section delivery
Gestational Diabetes
Glucose intolerance in pregnancy; OGTT screening at 24–28 weeks; diet + insulin; macrosomia risk
Polycystic Ovary Syndrome
Androgen excess + irregular cycles + polycystic ovaries; metabolic syndrome risk; metformin, OCPs
Prostate Cancer
Most common male cancer; PSA ↑; digital rectal exam; Gleason score; watchful waiting to radical prostatectomy
Cervical Cancer
HPV (strains 16, 18); Pap + HPV co-test; LEEP, cone biopsy, hysterectomy depending on stage; vaccine prevention

Why This Matters as a Nurse
Reproductive health spans obstetrics, oncology, urology, and primary care — all requiring sensitive, knowledgeable nursing
Maternal-newborn nursing is an entire specialty requiring deep A&P knowledge. Labor assessment, fetal monitoring, postpartum care, and neonatal assessment all depend on reproductive physiology.
Obstetric emergencies are fast, fatal, and require immediate nursing action. Postpartum hemorrhage, eclampsia, shoulder dystocia, and cord prolapse — you must recognize and respond in seconds.
Reproductive assessments require exceptional communication skills. Sexual health history, pelvic exams, and discussions about reproductive choices demand cultural sensitivity and trauma-informed care.
Medications during pregnancy carry unique risks. Every drug has a safety category; you must know which medications are contraindicated and counsel accordingly.
Reproductive cancers require sensitive nursing care across the continuum. Cervical, ovarian, uterine, breast, prostate, and testicular cancers involve intimate assessments and emotionally complex conversations.
