Overview

Drug classes that act on the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system to reduce vasoconstriction and circulating blood volume.

ACE Inhibitors

  • Captopril
  • Enalapril
  • Lisinopril
  • Benazepril

Names end in -pril. Block ACE → ↓ Ang II + ↑ bradykinin

ARBs

  • Losartan
  • Valsartan
  • Telmisartan

Names end in -sartan. Block AT1 receptors directly

Direct Renin Inhibitor

  • Aliskiren

Directly inhibits renin → blocks the entire RAAS cascade upstream

ARNi

  • Sacubitril /
  • Valsartan
  • (Entresto®)

ARB + neprilysin inhibitor → ↑ natriuretic peptides

Aldosterone Antagonist

  • Spironolactone

Blocks mineralocorticoid receptors in distal tubule and collecting duct

Mechanism of Action

How each drug class interferes with the RAAS pathway and the resulting physiologic effects.

Drug ClassMechanismKey Physiologic Effects
ACEi Inhibits ACE enzyme → blocks Ang I→Ang II conversion AND prevents bradykinin degradation ↓ Vasoconstriction, ↓ Aldosterone, ↓ Na+/water
↑ Bradykinin → vasodilation (but cough/angioedema risk)
↓ Efferent arteriole resistance → renoprotective
ARB Blocks AT1 receptors — Ang II is produced but cannot bind to its primary receptor ↓ Vasoconstriction, ↓ Aldosterone, ↓ Na+/water
No effect on bradykinin → much lower cough/angioedema risk
DRI Directly inhibits renin enzyme → blocks angiotensinogen→Ang I conversion (most upstream point) ↓ Ang I, Ang II, and Aldosterone → ↓ peripheral vascular resistance
ARNi Dual action: blocks AT1 receptors + inhibits neprilysin (prevents breakdown of natriuretic peptides, bradykinin, and Ang II) ↓ Vasoconstriction & aldosterone
↑ ANP/BNP → diuresis and vasodilation
↑ Bradykinin → vasodilation
Aldosterone Antagonist Blocks mineralocorticoid receptors in the distal tubule and cortical collecting duct ↓ Na+ reabsorption, ↓ K+ excretion → diuresis without K+ loss (K+-sparing)
⚠️

Important: Because neprilysin normally degrades Ang II, inhibiting it alone would worsen vasoconstriction — that’s why sacubitril is always paired with the ARB valsartan in Entresto®.

AT1 vs AT2 Receptor Effects

AT1 Receptor (Harmful Chronic Effects)

  • Vasoconstriction
  • ↑ Sympathetic activity
  • Aldosterone + ADH release
  • Vascular smooth muscle proliferation
  • Endothelial dysfunction
  • Platelet aggregation
  • Enhanced inflammation

AT2 Receptor (Protective Effects)

  • Vasodilation
  • Antiproliferative effects
  • Cardiovascular protection
✓

ARBs block AT1 but leave AT2 unaffected — the protective AT2 effects continue unimpeded.

Pharmacokinetics

Absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion profiles across the RAAS inhibitor classes.

Drug ClassAbsorptionDistributionMetabolismExcretion / Half-life
ACEi Prodrugs have ↑ bioavailability. Onset 15–60 min. Minimal protein binding. Modest Vd. Prodrugs hydrolyzed in liver to active form. Active drugs pass through unchanged. Primarily urine. Half-life 2–24 hrs.
ARB Rapidly absorbed orally. Bioavailability varies by drug. Protein binding 95–99%. Vd: 0.1 L/kg (candesartan) to 7 L/kg (telmisartan). Hepatic metabolism. Feces ~60%, urine ~35% (~4% unchanged). Half-life 2–24 hrs.
DRI (Aliskiren) Only 3% bioavailability. Poor oral absorption, worsens with high-fat meals. Protein binding ~50%. Extent of hepatic metabolism unknown. Feces (biliary, unchanged). Urine ~25% unchanged. Half-life 24 hrs.
Chemical Structure Notes

ACEi Functional Groups

  • Captopril — sulfhydryl group → antioxidant properties (1st in class)
  • Enalapril — prodrug, hydrolyzed to enalaprilat
  • Lisinopril — lysine derivative of enalaprilat
  • Benazepril, fosinopril, quinapril — long-acting prodrugs

ARB Functional Groups

  • All ARBs contain a tetrazole ring (4 nitrogens + 1 carbon)
  • Also contain 1–2 imidazole groups
  • Structural differences account for varying bioavailability and protein binding

Indications

FDA-approved and off-label uses across the RAAS inhibitor drug classes.

ACEi & ARB

FDA-Approved

  • Hypertension — 1st-line, especially with Type 2 DM, CKD, or CAD
  • Diabetic nephropathy
  • Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF)
  • STEMI — within 24 hrs improves survival in hemodynamically stable patients

Off-Label

  • Non-ST-elevation ACS
  • Stable CAD
  • Proteinuric CKD
  • Post-transplant erythrocytosis (renal transplant)
DRI — Aliskiren
  • Indicated for hypertension
  • Used as add-on therapy when already on other antihypertensives
  • Not recommended as initial/first-line treatment
ARNi — Entresto®
  • Indicated for chronic HFrEF
  • Shown to reduce cardiovascular death and hospitalizations in HFrEF
Prescribing Principles
  • ACEi and ARBs are NOT typically used together (except in rare circumstances, usually by nephrologists)
  • Both classes are frequently combined with diuretics (most often hydrochlorothiazide) or calcium channel blockers
  • ARBs are better tolerated than ACEis — patients are more likely to adhere
  • An ARB is preferred when a patient has ACEi-induced cough or angioedema history

Adverse Effects & Contraindications

ACEis carry a higher adverse effect burden than ARBs across nearly every category.

ACEi vs ARB Adverse Effect Comparison
Adverse EffectACEi RateARB RateMechanism (ACEi)
Hyperkalemia1%0.3%↓ Aldosterone → ↓ K+ excretion
Dry Cough10–20%1 / 1,000↑ Bradykinin irritates airways
Pancreatitis1 / 5,0001 / 15,000Mechanism unclear; risk 1/10 if prior ACEi pancreatitis
Angioedema1 / 2,0001 / 20,000↑ Bradykinin → tissue swelling; potentially fatal
Additional Side Effects
  • Hypotension
  • Dizziness / syncope
  • Headache
  • ↑ BUN / creatinine (rare: acute renal failure)
  • Diarrhea (specifically with aliskiren)
Absolute Contraindications
  • History of angioedema
  • Pregnancy — teratogenic (fetal renal injury)
  • Diabetic patients must NOT combine DRI with ACEi or ARB
Relative Contraindications
  • Abnormal renal function
  • Aortic stenosis — afterload reduction can cause severe hypotension
  • Dehydration / hypovolemia
  • Concurrent K+-sparing diuretics → ↑ hyperkalemia risk
🚨

Angioedema Warning: Angioedema from ACEis can involve the tongue, glottis, or larynx and may be life-threatening. A history of ACEi-induced angioedema is an absolute contraindication to restarting any ACEi — and is a relative contraindication for ARBs (ARB risk is 10× lower).

Side-by-side reference for the main antihypertensive classes, including RAAS inhibitors and complementary drug classes.

📌

Common Combinations: ACEi/ARB + thiazide diuretic is a frequent pairing for hypertension. ACEi/ARB + CCB is another standard combination. These work through complementary mechanisms and are often available as single combination pills to improve adherence.
